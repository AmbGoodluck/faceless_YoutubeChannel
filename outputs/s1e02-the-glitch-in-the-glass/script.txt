The Glitch in the Glass — Ep 2: A Red Top & A Haunted House

NARRATOR: Previously, Maya and her friends gathered in her old Victorian home. An antique mirror, a recent inheritance, had begun to show unsettling discrepancies. Maya’s own reflection seemed to move, to feel, independently. The group struggled to rationalize what they were seeing, but the mirror was only just beginning to reveal its nature.
CHLOE: Are we sure we're not just, you know, overtired? Or seeing things because we want to see things?
LIAM: I've been going over it in my head. Every angle. My reflection's never blinked separately from me before. It's just... a mirror.
SAM: Unless it's not. I've been doing some digging online about old mirrors, 'haunted' objects, even just rare antiques. Nothing concrete, but a lot of folklore.
MAYA: Folklore? Like, 'don't break it or seven years bad luck' folklore?
SAM: More like, 'mirrors can trap souls' or 'act as gateways' folklore. Honestly, it's all pretty outlandish, but then again, so is my reflection looking sad when I'm just annoyed.
CHLOE: Okay, so what do we do? Stare at it until it shows us something else?
MAYA: Maybe we just... leave it alone for a bit. See if it settles down.
LIAM: Ignoring it feels like the worst idea, Maya. What if it gets worse when we're not watching?
NARRATOR: Despite Liam's reservations, a few days passed with the mirror covered by a sheet in Maya's attic. Life seemed to return to a semblance of normal, at least on the surface. But the quiet hum of the unexplained lingered in the background, a low frequency only some of them could still perceive.
CHLOE: Hey, Maya, can I borrow your blue scarf? The one with the little tassels?
MAYA: Oh, sure, it's in my room, in the top drawer of the dresser. Help yourself.
NARRATOR: Chloe headed to Maya's bedroom, her mind on the simple task. As she walked past the large, antique mirror that still stood uncovered in the corner of Maya's room, she glanced at her reflection. For a fleeting instant, not even a full second, the Chloe in the glass was wearing a different top. A vivid red one, with an unfamiliar pattern, instead of the plain grey t-shirt she had on. Chloe stopped, a prickle running up her arms.
CHLOE: What...?
NARRATOR: She blinked, then stared intently. Her reflection was back to normal, the plain grey t-shirt mirroring her own perfectly. She slowly reached out a hand, touching the cold glass. Nothing. She shook her head, trying to dismiss it as a trick of the light, or her own imagination.
CHLOE: Just... a trick of the light.
NARRATOR: Later that evening, Liam was on a video call with Sam, discussing a new game. Liam's room was dimly lit, save for his monitor's glow. He gestured animatedly, describing a particularly difficult level. In the background, barely visible in the periphery of his webcam, was a framed poster on his wall.
LIAM: And then, just when I thought I had it, the boss totally blindsided me with that triple combo!
SAM: Dude, you gotta tell me which server you're on, I need to check out your build.
NARRATOR: As Liam leaned back in his chair, briefly out of the shot, a subtle shift occurred. The poster on his wall, a band poster for 'Crimson Reign,' subtly changed in its reflection on the monitor's glass. For a moment, the image of the band was replaced by a stark, black-and-white print of an old, dilapidated house. It was barely there, a ghost of an image, before the monitor's glare shifted, and it was gone. Liam leaned back into view, oblivious.
LIAM: No, seriously, this game is something else. You wouldn't believe the detail.
SAM: I can only imagine. My reflection just showed me an ad for a haunted house tour. I think the internet knows what we're talking about.
LIAM: Ha, probably. The algorithms are getting creepy these days.
NARRATOR: But the truth was, it wasn't the algorithms. It was the mirror. The changes were no longer confined to Maya's reflection, or even just to direct observations. The mirror's distortions were seeping into the reflections of the world around it, subtly altering what was seen, making the ordinary feel unsettlingly wrong. That night, as Maya slept, a faint, almost imperceptible shimmer pulsed from the covered mirror in her attic. The sheet over it rippled, not from a breeze, but from something within. And in her bedroom, the antique mirror, still standing uncovered, caught the moonlight. For a moment, its glass seemed to deepen, to draw in the light, becoming not a surface, but a void. What would it show next, now that its influence had begun to spread beyond the glass itself?
